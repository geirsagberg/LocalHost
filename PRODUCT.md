# Product

## Register

product

## Users

LocalHost Monitor is for developers working on local web projects on macOS. They use it while coding, testing, switching between dev servers, and cleaning up processes that are still listening on local ports. Their context is practical and interruption-sensitive: they want to see what is running, open the right local site, identify the owning process, hide noise, and stop a process without leaving their flow.

## Product Purpose

LocalHost Monitor discovers localhost websites from listening local TCP ports, presents the useful site facts in a native macOS window and menu bar extra, and lets users act on each site directly. Success means a developer can understand their local web environment at a glance, open or copy the right URL, customize names and emoji, hide entries from the default view, and terminate a listening process with confidence.

## Brand Personality

Quiet, sharp, native. The product should feel like a focused macOS utility: restrained, legible, fast, and trustworthy. It can carry a little developer-friendly warmth through details like deterministic emoji and helpful labels, but the interface should not perform for attention or drift away from platform conventions.

## Anti-references

Avoid marketing-site gloss, decorative dashboards, loud gradients, novelty controls, terminal-cosplay styling, and anything that makes local site management feel heavier than the task itself. Avoid generic server-monitoring language when the app is specifically about localhost sites and site preferences. Avoid visual treatments that fight native macOS affordances or make destructive actions feel casual.

## Design Principles

Make the local environment legible at a glance.

Keep the default view calm and useful, with deeper detail available through explicit hidden-site and non-OK controls.

Use native macOS patterns as the baseline for trust, speed, and accessibility.

Separate identity from metadata: titles, emoji, status, process name, and PID should remain distinct facts rather than collapsing into one overloaded label.

Treat destructive actions as precise tools, not dramatic moments.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast and preserve macOS accessibility conventions for focus, keyboard access, VoiceOver labeling, native controls, and system color behavior. Motion should be minimal and state-driven, with reduced-motion preferences respected. Color should never be the only cue for status, visibility, or destructive actions.
